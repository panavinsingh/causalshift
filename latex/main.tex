\begin{abstract}
When should an intelligent agent invest computational resources in causal reasoning
rather than relying on cheaper predictive approximations? We answer this question with
a constructive separation theorem. We define \emph{modular SCM-MDPs}---sequential
decision processes whose transitions are governed by structural causal models with
modular structure---and study \emph{graph-preserving shift families}, where the causal
graph is fixed but mechanism parameters change. Within this framework, we construct two
state abstractions of identical compression ratio that produce exactly the same
trajectory distribution on a source environment. Despite this observational
indistinguishability, we prove they exhibit radically different transfer behavior: the
predictive abstraction suffers linear regret under graph-preserving shift, while the
\emph{mechanism-invariant abstraction}---which respects the environment's causal
structure---achieves zero transfer regret. Crucially, no amount of source-environment
data can distinguish the two abstractions; only knowledge of the causal mechanism
structure separates them. We formalize the \emph{counterfactual cost} as the minimal
additional representational overhead required to upgrade from a source-adequate
abstraction to a mechanism-invariant one, providing the first operational measure of
what causal knowledge is worth in sequential decision-making. We complement our
theoretical results with \textsc{CausalShift}, a benchmark suite of modular
environments with graph-preserving perturbations, and demonstrate that an adaptive
metacontroller achieves a superior compute-regret Pareto frontier compared to purely
predictive baselines and frontier LLM planners (GPT-5.4, Claude~Opus~4.6,
Gemini~3.1~Pro). Our results formalize the intuition that general
intelligence---robustness to structured novelty---has an irreducible cost that
predictive learning alone cannot pay.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

A central question in artificial general intelligence is whether systems optimized for
prediction can achieve robust generalization across novel environments. Large language
models and world-model agents demonstrate remarkable in-distribution competence, yet
they remain fragile when deployed under distribution shift---precisely the conditions
that characterize general intelligence \citep{chollet2019measure, goertzel2014agi}.

We formalize this fragility as a provable separation between two types of state
abstraction in modular sequential decision processes. Our main result (\Cref{thm:separation})
shows that \emph{two abstractions that are observationally identical on a source
environment can have arbitrarily different transfer behavior under structured shift}.
The key difference: one abstraction preserves the causal mechanism structure of the
environment (mechanism-invariant), while the other merely preserves predictive
statistics (predictive).

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Separation Theorem.} We prove a constructive separation between
    mechanism-invariant and predictive abstractions: identical source behavior, linear
    vs.\ zero transfer regret (\Cref{thm:separation}).

    \item \textbf{Counterfactual Cost.} We define $\Ccf$, the minimal representational
    overhead for mechanism invariance---the first operational measure of what causal
    knowledge is worth in sequential decision-making (\Cref{def:ccf}).

    \item \textbf{CausalShift Benchmark + Adaptive Metacontrol.} A benchmark suite
    with graph-preserving shifts and an EXP3-based router that selectively engages
    causal reasoning, achieving a superior compute-regret Pareto frontier
    (\Cref{sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}
%% ============================================================

\begin{definition}[Modular SCM-MDP]
\label{def:scm-mdp}
A \emph{modular SCM-MDP} is a tuple $\cM = (\cS, \cA, \cG, \{f_v\}_{v \in \cG}, R)$
where $\cS = \cS_1 \times \cdots \times \cS_K$ is a factored state space with $K$
components, $\cA$ is the action space, $\cG$ is a directed acyclic graph over the
components defining causal dependencies, $f_v : \text{Pa}(v) \times \cA \times
\mathcal{U}_v \to \cS_v$ are structural mechanisms for each node $v$ with exogenous
noise $\mathcal{U}_v$, and $R : \cS \times \cA \to [0,1]$ is the reward function.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Graph-Preserving Shift Family]
\label{def:shift-family}
A \emph{graph-preserving shift family} is a set $\cF_\cG = \{\cM_\theta : \theta \in
\Theta\}$ of modular SCM-MDPs sharing the same causal graph $\cG$ but differing in
mechanism parameters $\theta$. All members share the same state space, action space,
and reward function; only the structural mechanisms $\{f_v^\theta\}$ vary.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Mechanism-Invariant Abstraction]
\label{def:mech-inv}
An abstraction $\phi : \cS \to \cZ$ is \emph{mechanism-invariant} over a shift family
$\cF_\cG$ if the abstract transition kernel is identical across all members:
\[
    P_{\cM_\theta}(Z', R \mid Z, A) = P_{\cM_{\theta'}}(Z', R \mid Z, A)
    \quad \forall \theta, \theta' \in \Theta, \; \forall Z \in \cZ, A \in \cA.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Source-Adequate Abstraction]
\label{def:source-adequate}
An abstraction $\phi$ is \emph{source-adequate} for $\cM_0$ if an optimal policy in the
abstract MDP induced by $\phi$ achieves near-optimal value:
$V^*(\cM_0, \phi) \geq V^*(\cM_0) - \epsilon$ for small $\epsilon > 0$.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Protocol $\mathcal{P}$.} Phase~1: the agent interacts with $\cM_0$ for $n$
episodes, observing only abstract trajectories $(Z_t, A_t, R_t, Z_{t+1})$ under a
\emph{fixed} abstraction $\phi$. Phase~2: the agent is deployed on $\cM_{\theta'}$
(unknown $\theta'$) under the \emph{same} $\phi$. We measure transfer regret
$\Regret(\phi, \cM_{\theta'}, T) = T \cdot V^*(\cM_{\theta'}) - \sum_{t=1}^T R_t$.

%% ============================================================
\section{The Separation Theorem}
\label{sec:theorem}
%% ============================================================

\begin{theorem}[Counterfactual Separation]
\label{thm:separation}
There exists a modular SCM-MDP family $\cF_\cG$ and two abstractions $\phim$
(mechanism-invariant) and $\phip$ (source-adequate but not mechanism-invariant) with
$|\cZ_{\phim}| = |\cZ_{\phip}|$ and identical abstract trajectory distributions under
$\cM_0$, such that under Protocol~$\mathcal{P}$, for any measurable learner operating
on abstract trajectories:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(a)] $\Regret(\phim, \cM_{\theta'}, T) = 0$ for all
    $\cM_{\theta'} \in \cF_\cG$;
    \item[(b)] $\Regret(\phip, \cM_{\theta'}, T) = \Omega(|\theta' - \theta_0| \cdot T)$
    for $\theta' \neq \theta_0$;
    \item[(c)] The Phase~1 abstract trajectory distributions under $\phim$ and $\phip$
    are identical---no measurable function of source data can distinguish them.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\paragraph{Construction.} We define a 2-component modular SCM-MDP with $S = (S_1, S_2)
\in \{0,1\}^2$, causal graph $S_1 \to S_2$, and mechanisms:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $S_1(t+1) \sim \text{Bernoulli}(1/2)$ (exogenous, iid);
    \item $S_2(t+1) = S_1(t+1) \oplus B_\theta$, \; $B_\theta \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\theta)$;
    \item Action $A \in \{0,1\}$, reward $R = \mathbf{1}[A = S_1]$.
\end{itemize}
The shift family is $\cF_\cG = \{\cM_\theta : \theta \in [0, 0.5]\}$ with source
$\theta_0 = 0$. The abstractions are $\phim(S) = S_1$ and $\phip(S) = S_2$.

\paragraph{Proof sketch.} At $\theta_0 = 0$: $S_2 = S_1$, so $\phim$ and $\phip$ are
identical mappings. Both induce the same abstract MDP and the same optimal policy
$\pi^*(Z) = Z$.

Under shift $\theta' > 0$: $\phim(S) = S_1$ still produces the correct abstract state
for the policy $\pi^*(Z) = Z = S_1$, yielding $R = 1$ at every step. The abstract MDP
under $\phim$ is \emph{fully invariant}: both transition dynamics and reward are
independent of $\theta$.

For $\phip(S) = S_2 = S_1 \oplus B_{\theta'}$: the policy $\pi^*(Z) = Z = S_2$ yields
$R = \mathbf{1}[S_2 = S_1]$. With probability $\theta'$, $B = 1$ and $S_2 \neq S_1$,
so the action mismatches. Expected reward per step: $1 - \theta'$. Total regret over
$T$ steps: $\theta' \cdot T = \Omega(|\theta' - \theta_0| \cdot T)$.

Indistinguishability: under $\theta_0 = 0$, $\phim(S) = S_1 = S_2 = \phip(S)$ for
every state realization. The abstract trajectory distributions are \emph{exactly}
identical---not merely close in total variation, but literally the same probability
measure. \hfill $\square$

\section{The Counterfactual Cost}
\label{sec:ccf}
%% ============================================================

\begin{definition}[Counterfactual Cost]
\label{def:ccf}
Given a shift family $\cF_\cG$ and a function class $\mathcal{F}_d$ of abstractions
mapping to $d$-dimensional abstract spaces, the \emph{counterfactual cost} is:
\[
    \Ccf(\cG, \mathcal{F}_d) = \min_{\substack{\phi \in \mathcal{F}_d \\ \text{mech-invariant}}}
    \text{complexity}(\phi) - \min_{\substack{\phi \in \mathcal{F}_d \\ \text{source-adequate}}}
    \text{complexity}(\phi),
\]
where complexity is measured as description length within $\mathcal{F}_d$.
\end{definition}

In our construction, both $\phim$ and $\phip$ are 1-bit abstractions, so $\Ccf = 0$ in
terms of abstract state cardinality. The cost is not in \emph{how much} information the
abstraction retains, but in \emph{which} information. This is precisely the sense in
which standard information-theoretic measures fail to capture transfer robustness: two
abstractions with identical entropy and mutual information with reward under the source
distribution can have radically different transfer properties.

\section{Adaptive Metacontrol}
\label{sec:router}
%% ============================================================

Given access to both $\phim$ and $\phip$, a natural question is: when should the agent
invest in the mechanism-invariant abstraction? We formalize this as an online selection
problem and propose an EXP3-based router that adaptively allocates between the two
abstractions.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:router}
The adaptive router achieves additive regret $O(\sqrt{T \log 2})$ against the best
fixed abstraction in hindsight, while the random router incurs regret
$\frac{\theta' T}{2}$.
\end{proposition}

In our experiments, the adaptive router recovers 93\% of the gap between always-predictive
and always-mechanism-invariant at $\theta' = 0.5$, while using $\phim$ for only 93\%
of steps (saving 7\% of compute when shift is detected to be high).

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%% ============================================================

We validate the separation theorem and adaptive metacontrol across three environments of
increasing structural complexity, 15 baselines including 3 frontier LLMs, and 30
random seeds per configuration.

\subsection{CausalShift Benchmark}

\paragraph{CausalShift-XOR.} The theorem's constructive environment (2 components,
binary, $S_1 \to S_2$). Shift parameter: $\theta \in [0, 0.5]$.

\paragraph{CausalShift-Chain.} 5-component binary chain ($X_1 \to X_2 \to \cdots \to
X_5$). Shift: noise $p_3$ at the middle mechanism $X_2 \to X_3$. Tests whether the
separation scales through causal depth.

\paragraph{CausalShift-Branch.} 4 observed components with hidden confounder $U$.
Branching DAG: $X_1 \to \{X_2, X_3\}$, $\{X_2, X_3\} \to X_4$, $U \to \{X_2, X_3\}$.
Shift: confounder distribution $\mu$. Tests whether confounders amplify the separation.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Causal abstraction.}
\citet{beckers2019abstracting} defined approximate causal abstraction as structure-preserving
maps between causal models. \citet{geiger2021causal} developed interchange intervention
training for neural networks. Our work differs by proving a \emph{transfer regret
separation}, not an alignment result about interpretation.

\paragraph{Bisimulation in RL.}
\citet{li2006towards} introduced bisimulation for state abstraction in MDPs;
\citet{zhang2021learning} extended this to deep bisimulation for control (DBC).
\citet{wang2024building} proposed Causal Bisimulation Modeling (CBM) for task-specific
causal abstractions. Our theorem shows that predictive bisimulation is provably
insufficient under graph-preserving shift.

\paragraph{World models.}
DreamerV3 \citep{hafner2023mastering}, Dreamer-CDP \citep{zenke2026dreamercpd}, and
V-JEPA~2 learn latent dynamics models. Our result provides theoretical justification
for incorporating causal structure into world models rather than relying on purely
predictive latent representations.

\paragraph{AGI and the evaluation of generality.}
The AGI conference has long studied what makes intelligence general
\citep{legg2007collection, wang2008what, chollet2019measure}. \citet{omohundro2008basic}
identified convergent instrumental drives; we identify a convergent \emph{representational}
requirement for generality: mechanism invariance.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Implications for LLM-based agents.} Our result suggests that systems trained
exclusively on observational data may learn abstractions that are predictively adequate
but not mechanism-invariant, implying fragile transfer. This is a hypothesis motivated
by our theorem, not a direct consequence---LLMs may approximate mechanism invariance
through other means (e.g., language-encoded causal knowledge).

\paragraph{Limitations.} The constructive proof uses a minimal 2-component SCM-MDP.
While we demonstrate scaling to 5-component chains and branching DAGs with confounders,
the environments remain relatively simple compared to real-world settings. The
counterfactual cost $\Ccf$ is model-class-relative, not absolute.

\paragraph{Broader impact.} Understanding when causal reasoning is necessary for
robust intelligence has implications for AI safety: systems that appear competent
in-distribution but lack mechanism-invariant representations may fail unpredictably
under deployment shift.
